```

  │                                                                 │

  │                                                                 │

```

BirTics was formed with one purpose: to compete in **WRO Future Engineers 2026**.

But from the very first conversation, we knew we wanted to do something most teams don't do — we wanted to **build the documentation as seriously as we build the robot.**

We are three students from Birzeit University: Julnar, Lana, and Nadeen. We each bring a different kind of engineering thinking to the table. And we chose to combine those perspectives into a single, unified team.

The name *BirTics* carries both our roots — **Bir**zeit — and our direction: the practice of engineering as a discipline, not just a task.

## 💡 &nbsp;The Conversation That Changed Everything

It didn't start with hardware specs or component lists. It started with a question about **autonomous vehicles**.

```
  Early Team Discussion — How It Went
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  "How does a self-driving car decide when to turn?"

       ↓

  "It depends on what sensors it has."

       ↓

  "But how do you choose the sensors?"

       ↓

  "It depends on what decisions the software needs to make."

       ↓

  "But how do you choose the software architecture?"

       ↓

  "It depends on the physical constraints of the robot."

       ↓

  "So... everything depends on everything else."

       ↓

  "Yes. And that's exactly what we need to document."
```

That recursive loop — where every engineering decision feeds into every other — became the foundation of how BirTics works.

We realized that **the hardest part of building an autonomous robot isn't any single component.** It's understanding how all the components talk to each other, and making deliberate choices about those relationships.

From that moment, we committed to something: **every major decision would be reasoned, recorded, and reviewable.**

## 🧭 &nbsp;Our Engineering Philosophy

```
  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │                                                                 │
  │   THE BIRTICTS PRINCIPLES                                       │
  │   ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────      │
  │                                                                 │
  │   01 · Document before you build.                               │
  │        Write down what you expect to happen — then test it.     │
  │                                                                 │
  │   02 · A failed test is not a failure.                          │
  │        It is data. Log it, learn from it, iterate.              │
  │                                                                 │
  │   03 · Every decision has a reason.                             │
  │        If you can't explain why, you're not ready to decide.    │
  │                                                                 │
  │   04 · The robot reflects the team.                             │
  │        Disorganized thinking produces disorganized engineering. │
  │                                                                 │
  │   05 · Autonomy is earned, not assumed.                         │
  │        The robot earns the right to drive itself                │
  │        by proving it can handle each scenario first.            │
  │                                                                 │
  └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

These principles came out of those first conversations — not from a textbook, but from asking ourselves *what kind of engineers do we want to be?*

We are students. We will make mistakes. We will hit dead ends. We will have to restart subsystems, rethink assumptions, and redo work we thought was finished.

**That's not a problem. That's the process. And we're documenting all of it.**
